External User Analytics: What You Need to Know

Deploying analytics to external users refers to the process of making analytical data and insights accessible to stakeholders outside your business: clients, partners, vendors, or customers. This is often done through visual tools such as dashboards and reports. Unlike internal analytics that focus on improving business operations or decision-making, external user analytics primarily serve to improve customer experiences, service offerings, or report performance metrics

In this data-driven age, external user analytics have emerged as a game-changer. Businesses use them not just to inform and engage key external stakeholders but also to scale and innovate.

They can shape services, add value to customer journeys, or make collaborations more transparent and rewarding. However, delivering effective external user analytics is a challenging feat; one that demands a careful balance between the richness of insights, ease of use and strict adherence to data privacy regulations such as GDPR and HIPAA.This guide aims to arm you with tried-and-true best practices that can make your analytics deployment mission a success. Stick with us, and you’ll learn to optimize tools like Power BI Embedded and branded Power BI dashboards with confidence.

Utilizing Branded Power BI Dashboards for a Personalized Experience

‍

An effective analytics deployment strategy revolves around delivering the right information to the external users in a manner that resonates with their experience.

‍

Branded Power BI dashboards stand out as a key facilitator in this regard. One of the main advantages of using these dashboards is their high degree of customization.

The power to tailor the dashboard’s look and feel according to the brand’s style guide can lead to a stronger sense of consistency and engagement with external users. This can range from workflows designed to the brand’s specifications to theming options that tie in closely with the overall brand identity.

‍

‍

Another edge to branded dashboards is their potential to enhance the analytics experience for external users through custom themes, JSON templates, and tenant-level branding options in Power BI.

‍

For example, a well-designed, branded dashboard can take your raw data and transform it into actionable insights that external users can easily understand and interact with. Furthermore, they can present the data in a visually appealing format that aligns with the brand’s image, promoting higher interaction and engagement from the users.

‍

‍However, it’s important to strike the right balance during personalization. Over-customization can lead to complex interfaces and slower performance, while too little might not effectively communicate the intended message.

Discussing Power BI Licensing for External Users

Power BI’s licensing model for external users is built to accommodate flexibility, user growth, and optimize cost-efficiency. Essentially, Microsoft’s Power BI service is offered through multiple licensing models: user-based (Pro, Premium per User) and capacity-based (Premium capacity, Embedded SKUs).

‍

The user-based model, Power BI Pro

is intended for content creators and professionals who need to build and share reports with others. Each user needs a license, making this model perfect for a company with a small and defined number of external users to serve. However, for an expanding enterprise or when large-scale deployments to numerous external users are involved, this model may not offer the best financial sense.

The Power BI Premium

is capacity-based and adds enterprise features like larger datasets, paginated reports, and advanced AI capabilities. Rather than purchasing per-user licenses, organizations can scale resources according to their needs and offer access to a large number of external users. This model offers financial predictability and flexibility, ensuring that organizations only pay for the capacity they actually use. In terms of cost management, this model allows for usage optimization by increasing or decreasing capacity based on necessity.

‍Effectively managing Power BI licensing lies in understanding the unique needs of your enterprise. Assess your organization’s data needs, number of users, growth projections, and budget. This will enable you to decide on a licensing model that optimizes cost and ensures the efficient use of resources. Working with a Microsoft-certified consultant or leveraging resources such as Microsoft’s capacity planning tool can also be beneficial in making an informed decision.

Remember, each licensing model has its strengths and ideal use case scenarios. By understanding these, your organization can maximize the benefits of Power BI’s flexible licensing, adapt to business transformations, and ensure the continuous, cost-effective deployment of analytics to your external users.

The Role of Multi-Tenant Power BI Architecture in Scaling Analytics

Deploying analytics to a growing number of external users needs a flexible, scalable solution. That’s where the concept of multi-tenant Power BI architecture becomes significant.

In layman’s terms, multi-tenancy in Power BI embedded refers to designing one workspace/app to securely serve multiple clients (tenants) using row-level security (RLS) and capacity management.Think of it as an apartment building. The building (Power BI instance) is shared by multiple residents (users). Each resident has their own secure, customized living space (dashboards and reports). This allows for resources to be maximized while keeping costs relatively low.

Scaling analytics with a multi-tenant Power BI architecture is beneficial as it uses resources efficiently, lowers maintenance and infrastructure costs, and swiftly adapts to growing user bases. It also provides the ability to deliver tailored user experiences with shared resources.

‍

For instance, in a multi-tenant environment, if a bug is fixed or a new feature is added, the change is immediately reflected across all tenants, meaning improved updates and less downtime for your external users. Furthermore, because the infrastructure is shared and centralized, it’s possible to implement wide-reaching security protocols.

Microsoft provides in-depth guidance on creating and managing such an environment through its shared guidance. Following these protocols can help optimize and streamline your deployment strategy.

So, to scale analytics successfully, it’s important to consider the multi-tenant approach when deploying Power BI, enabling more effective performance, cost optimization, and seamless experiences for external users. Just remember, every tenant in your ‘analytics apartment building’ has unique needs, and effective multi-tenant architecture can help you cater to these needs efficiently and effectively.
